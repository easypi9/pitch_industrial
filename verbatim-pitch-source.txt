#2 - Problem
Industrial subcontracting is still managed like a pre-digital process.
The bottleneck isn’t manufacturing capacity — it’s coordination and trust
across multiple parties.
Supplier discovery is unstructured and relationship-driven
(finding the right capability for a specific scope still depends
on “who you know”)
Offers arrive in inconsistent formats
hard to compare
(procurement teams spend time normalizing information instead of
making decisions)
Supplier verification is fragmented
(capabilities, compliance and documentation are scattered and
repeatedly re-checked)
Execution + documentation are spread across disconnected tools
(no single operational thread from RFQ to close-out)
Decision-making is slow and opaque on both sides.
Buyers wait days or weeks for technically validated offers.
Suppliers spend days internally assessing feasibility — checking
material availability, machine capacity, process compatibility,
workforce load, deadline feasibility (DL), and pricing assumptions
across disconnected systems and teams.

Technical validation is manual and iterative.
Engineering, production planning, procurement, and management
exchange multiple internal iterations before an offer can even be
submitted.
Capacity visibility is unclear.
There is no real-time structured view of production windows,
bottlenecks, or available manufacturing bandwidth.
Logistics, installation, service providers, and warehousing are
coordinated separately
, often only after award — creating
downstream delays, cost overruns, and execution risk.
Decision speed is constrained by:
manual RFQ interpretation
spreadsheet-based cost calculations
repeated material and technology checks
internal approval loops
disconnected logistics coordination
As markets accelerate, slow coordination becomes structural
disadvantage.
The result:
long cycle times
high internal labor cost per RFQ
rework and misalignment
delayed downstream logistics planning
risk exposure
and no compounding data advantage
Industrial subcontracting today is not limited by machines —
it is limited by the speed and structure of decision-making.

#10 - Business Model: Infrastructure Monetization
INDUSTRIAL monetizes like infrastructure: predictable software
revenue plus transaction-linked upside as the network scales.
Buyer side (recurring, predictable)
SaaS subscription priced by active projects / workspaces
Value delivered: operational control, standardized workflow,
governance (roles/permissions), verification, auditability,
reporting
Enterprise path: onboarding + integrations + advanced analytics
(optional)
Supplier side (aligned incentives, low adoption friction)
Success-based fee (percentage) applied only when winning awarded
work through the platform
Rationale: suppliers do not pay “for air”; they pay when the
platform produces revenue outcomes
Why a hybrid model is structurally advantaged for an infrastructure
platform
Reduces onboarding friction on the supply side (critical for
network formation)
Keeps revenue stable via SaaS while enabling significant upside via
transaction volume
Aligns incentives across both sides and strengthens network effects
Long-term outcome
As the verified supply graph and structured offer/performance data
compound, INDUSTRIAL becomes the default subcontracting interface and the
transaction layer scales with usage.
